THE PROBLEM

Hospital waiting times create anxiety

NHS hospital wards face a persistent challenge: patients waiting hours without clear information about when they'll be seen. This uncertainty creates anxiety, leads to frequent staff interruptions, and compounds existing patient stress.

Our EdTech startup partnered with three NHS trusts in Birmingham to explore whether gamification could reduce perceived wait times and improve patient experience — without changing actual queue processing times.

Key insight: The problem wasn't the wait itself — it was the uncertainty. Patients consistently rated 60-minute waits with real-time updates as better experiences than 45-minute waits with no information.

THE APPROACH

Gamification meets real-time updates

I architected a real-time queue management system with three core features:

  • Live position tracking: Patients see their queue position update in real-time as others are seen
  • Gamified waiting: Simple browser-based puzzles and trivia to occupy time productively
  • Smart notifications: SMS alerts when their turn is approaching (15-minute warning)

The system integrated with existing NHS patient management systems via HL7 FHIR APIs, ensuring no disruption to clinical workflows. Ward staff continued using their existing systems — Wait & Play simply provided a patient-facing layer.

CHALLENGES

Three hard problems I solved

Challenge 1

HIPAA compliance and data security

NHS patient data requires end-to-end encryption and strict access controls. Implemented zero-knowledge architecture where patient identifiers are hashed on device before transmission. Server only stores anonymised queue positions, never linking them to patient records. Passed NHS Digital security audit on first submission.

Challenge 2

Real-time accuracy at scale

WebSocket connections can drop, creating stale queue positions that destroy trust. Built automatic reconnection with position verification via PostgreSQL polling fallback. If WebSocket fails, system polls REST endpoint every 30s as backup. Achieved 99.2% uptime across three wards handling 2,400 patients/month.

Challenge 3

Elderly patient accessibility

Initial testing revealed 40% of elderly patients couldn't use the system due to small text and complex UI. Redesigned with WCAG AAA contrast ratios, 18px minimum font size, and voice-guided navigation. Added large-print kiosk mode for ward tablets. Post-redesign usability score increased from 58% to 91%.

RESULTS

What changed after deployment

After 12 months running across three NHS pilot wards:

  • 37% reduction in perceived wait times (measured via post-visit surveys)
  • 28% increase in patient satisfaction scores
  • 45% decrease in "how much longer?" interruptions to ward staff
  • 2,400 patients/month now using the system voluntarily
  • Zero clinical workflow disruption — staff reported no change to their processes

Most surprising outcome: 67% of patients reported that the puzzle games "made the wait feel shorter" even when they didn't actually play them — just knowing they could reduced anxiety.

WHAT I'D DO DIFFERENTLY

Lessons for next time

  • Start with accessibility: Retrofitting WCAG compliance cost 3 weeks. Should have designed for elderly users from day one, not as an afterthought.
  • Better HL7 FHIR documentation: NHS integration docs are sparse. Spent 2 weeks reverse-engineering API behaviour. Now maintain internal docs for team.
  • SMS costs at scale: Twilio bills added up fast at 2,400 patients/month. Should have negotiated bulk rates earlier. Lesson: always project costs at 10x scale.
  • Don't over-engineer gamification: Initial version had leaderboards and achievements. Users ignored them. Simple puzzles were enough. Sometimes less is more.
